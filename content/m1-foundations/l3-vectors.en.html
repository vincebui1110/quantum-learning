A quantum state is a vector. To work with states you need column vectors, the Dirac notation that physicists write them in, and one operation - the inner product - that measures how much two states overlap. This lesson assembles that toolkit and connects it straight to \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\).

Column vectors and the ket

For a single qubit, a state lives in a two-dimensional complex space \(\mathbb{C}^2\). We write states as column vectors, and Dirac's ket notation \(|\psi\rangle\) is just a name for such a column:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix} = \alpha\,|0\rangle + \beta\,|1\rangle, \qquad |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \]

The entries \(\alpha,\beta\) are complex numbers. Reading the ket as "a labeled column vector" is all you need: \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) are two specific columns, and every state is a combination of them.

The bra: turning a column into a row

To each ket \(|\psi\rangle\) we associate a bra \(\langle\psi|\), obtained by taking the conjugate transpose (also called the dagger, \(\dagger\)): lay the column on its side to make a row, and complex-conjugate every entry.

\[ |\psi\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \langle\psi| = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha^* & \beta^* \end{pmatrix}. \]

The conjugation is not optional decoration - it is exactly what makes lengths come out real and non-negative, as we are about to see.

Why "bra" and "ket"

Dirac split the word "bracket". A bra \(\langle\phi|\) and a ket \(|\psi\rangle\) placed together close up into a bracket \(\langle\phi|\psi\rangle\) - the inner product. The notation is designed so the pieces literally snap together.

The inner product

The inner product of \(|\phi\rangle\) and \(|\psi\rangle\) is the bra-ket product \(\langle\phi|\psi\rangle\): multiply the row by the column in the usual matrix way. With \(|\phi\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}\gamma\\\delta\end{pmatrix}\) and \(|\psi\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}\alpha\\\beta\end{pmatrix}\),

\[ \langle\phi|\psi\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}\gamma^* & \delta^*\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\alpha\\\beta\end{pmatrix} = \gamma^*\alpha + \delta^*\beta. \]

The result is a single complex number that measures the overlap between the two states. Setting \(|\phi\rangle = |\psi\rangle\) gives the state's own squared length:

\[ \langle\psi|\psi\rangle = \alpha^*\alpha + \beta^*\beta = |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2, \]

which is real and non-negative for every state - the payoff of conjugating in the bra.

Orthonormal basis and normalization

The states \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) satisfy two clean relations:

\[ \langle 0|0\rangle = 1, \quad \langle 1|1\rangle = 1, \quad \langle 0|1\rangle = 0. \]

Each has unit length (normalized) and they are perpendicular (orthogonal). A set with both properties is an orthonormal basis - the quantum analogue of perpendicular coordinate axes of length one. Every qubit state is a combination of this basis, and the amplitudes are just the coordinates.

A physical state must have unit norm:

\[ \|\psi\| = \sqrt{\langle\psi|\psi\rangle} = 1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1. \]

This is exactly the normalization rule from Module 2, now derived from the inner product. It says the total probability across all outcomes is one.

Worked example

Take \(|\psi\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|0\rangle + |1\rangle\big) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}\). Its bra is \(\langle\psi| = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\end{pmatrix}\). Then

\[ \langle\psi|\psi\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\,(1\cdot 1 + 1\cdot 1) = \tfrac12(2) = 1, \]

so \(\|\psi\| = 1\): the state is normalized. Its overlap with \(|0\rangle\) is \(\langle 0|\psi\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(1\cdot 1 + 0\cdot 1) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\), whose squared magnitude \(\tfrac12\) is precisely the probability of measuring \(0\).

Key idea

A qubit state is a unit column vector \(|\psi\rangle\); its bra \(\langle\psi|\) is the conjugate transpose; the inner product \(\langle\phi|\psi\rangle\) measures overlap. \(|0\rangle,|1\rangle\) form an orthonormal basis, and normalization is just \(\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 1\).

Key takeaways

  • A ket \(|\psi\rangle\) is a column vector; \(|0\rangle,|1\rangle\) are the two basis columns.
  • The bra \(\langle\psi|\) is the conjugate transpose (row, entries conjugated).
  • The inner product \(\langle\phi|\psi\rangle = \gamma^*\alpha + \delta^*\beta\) is a complex number measuring overlap.
  • \(|0\rangle,|1\rangle\) are orthonormal, and normalization \(\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = |\alpha|^2+|\beta|^2 = 1\) is a unit-norm condition.
