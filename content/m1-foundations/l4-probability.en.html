Quantum mechanics is a probabilistic theory: measuring a qubit gives a random outcome. It is tempting to conclude that a qubit is "just a coin we haven't looked at yet." That intuition is wrong, and understanding why is the sharpest dividing line between classical and quantum thinking. The difference comes down to one word: amplitudes.

Classical probability: a quick refresher

A classical random bit is described by a probability distribution: a list of non-negative numbers that sum to one. For a single bit,

\[ p_0 \ge 0, \quad p_1 \ge 0, \quad p_0 + p_1 = 1. \]

Here \(p_0\) is the probability of \(0\) and \(p_1\) of \(1\). Two facts define the classical world. Probabilities are never negative, and to combine independent ways of reaching an outcome you simply add the probabilities. Because you only ever add non-negative numbers, the chance of an outcome can only grow or hold as you find more paths to it - it can never shrink to zero through combination.

Quantum: amplitudes come first, probabilities second

A qubit is described not by probabilities but by complex amplitudes:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha\,|0\rangle + \beta\,|1\rangle, \qquad \alpha,\beta \in \mathbb{C}. \]

Amplitudes are the fundamental quantity, and they carry information a probability cannot: a sign and, more generally, a complex phase. Probabilities are derived from them, one squaring step later, by the Born rule:

\[ \Pr(\text{outcome } 0) = |\alpha|^2, \qquad \Pr(\text{outcome } 1) = |\beta|^2. \]

Normalization \(|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1\) guarantees these are a valid distribution. Squaring a magnitude always yields something non-negative, so the probabilities behave classically - but the amplitudes underneath do not.

The crucial difference

Classical states combine probabilities, which are non-negative and can only add. Quantum states combine amplitudes, which are complex and can cancel. Probabilities can never interfere; amplitudes can.

Interference: where the two worlds part

The single most important consequence is that amplitudes for the same outcome can point in "opposite directions" and destroy each other. Suppose two computational paths both lead to a given outcome, one contributing amplitude \(+\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\) and the other \(-\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\). You do not add their probabilities. You add their amplitudes first, then square:

\[ \left| \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2} + \left(-\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\right) \right|^2 = |0|^2 = 0. \]

The outcome becomes impossible. This is destructive interference, and nothing like it can happen classically: a classical calculation would give \(\tfrac12 + \tfrac12 = 1\), full certainty, the exact opposite answer. Constructive interference is the mirror image - two amplitudes with the same sign reinforce, pushing a probability up.

Worked example

Compare a classical mixture with a quantum superposition, both "50/50" in the standard basis.

Classical. A coin is 0 or 1 with \(p_0 = p_1 = \tfrac12\). If you could route it through two stages that each reshuffle, the probabilities just keep averaging; the randomness never sharpens back into certainty.

Quantum. The state \(|+\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\) also measures 50/50. But its amplitudes carry signs. A well-chosen operation can steer the two amplitudes for the "0" outcome to add (\(+\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}+\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\) contributions building up) while the two for "1" cancel - collapsing the 50/50 back to a certain 0. Recovering certainty from a superposition is a signature no classical coin can fake.

Looking ahead

The Born rule - probability equals squared amplitude - is the bridge from the abstract state vector to what a measurement actually reports. The next module studies measurement in full; here the point is only that squaring is where phase information gets discarded, which is why phase must do its work before you measure.

Key takeaways

  • Classical bits carry probabilities: non-negative numbers summing to one, which only ever add.
  • Qubits carry complex amplitudes; probabilities come from them via the Born rule, \(\Pr = |\text{amplitude}|^2\).
  • Amplitudes can cancel (destructive interference); probabilities never can - this is the core quantum difference.
  • You add amplitudes first and square second; squaring first (the classical move) misses interference.
