Quantum amplitudes are complex numbers, so the whole subject rests on a small amount of complex arithmetic. The good news: you need far less than a full course. This lesson gives you exactly the pieces that reappear on every later page - and no more.

The imaginary unit and the form a + bi

The imaginary unit \(i\) is defined by a single property:

\[ i^2 = -1. \]

There is no real number whose square is negative, so \(i\) is a genuinely new object. A general complex number is written

\[ z = a + bi, \qquad a,b \in \mathbb{R}, \]

where \(a\) is the real part and \(b\) the imaginary part. You add and multiply complex numbers just like ordinary algebra, using \(i^2 = -1\) whenever it appears. For example \((1+i)(2-i) = 2 - i + 2i - i^2 = 2 + i + 1 = 3 + i.\)

Complex conjugate, modulus, and |z|²

The complex conjugate of \(z = a + bi\) flips the sign of the imaginary part:

\[ z^* = a - bi. \]

The modulus (or magnitude) \(|z|\) measures the distance from \(z\) to the origin in the complex plane. Its square has an especially clean form, and it is this squared quantity that the Born rule will turn into a probability:

\[ |z|^2 = z\,z^* = (a+bi)(a-bi) = a^2 + b^2, \qquad |z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}. \]

Notice \(z z^*\) is always a non-negative real number, even though \(z\) itself is complex. That is exactly why quantum theory squares amplitudes to get probabilities: the squaring launders a complex amplitude into a legitimate, real, non-negative number.

Worked example

Let \(z = 3 + 4i\). Then

\[ z^* = 3 - 4i, \qquad |z|^2 = z z^* = 3^2 + 4^2 = 9 + 16 = 25, \qquad |z| = \sqrt{25} = 5. \]

If this were a (yet-to-be-normalized) amplitude, the unnormalized weight of its outcome would be \(25\). Compare a second amplitude \(w = i\): here \(|w|^2 = 0^2 + 1^2 = 1\), so \(w = i\) has full unit weight despite having no real part at all.

Polar form and Euler's formula

Every complex number can also be written by its distance from the origin and its angle. If \(r = |z|\) and \(\theta\) is the angle from the positive real axis, then

\[ z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta). \]

The bracket has a famously compact name via Euler's formula:

\[ e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta, \qquad\text{so}\qquad z = r\,e^{i\theta}. \]

A crucial consequence: since \(\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1\), the number \(e^{i\theta}\) always sits on the unit circle, meaning

\[ \left| e^{i\theta} \right| = 1 \quad\text{for every real } \theta. \]
Sanity check

Setting \(\theta = \pi\) gives \(e^{i\pi} = \cos\pi + i\sin\pi = -1\), the celebrated identity \(e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0\). Setting \(\theta = \pi/2\) gives \(e^{i\pi/2} = i\).

Why phase matters

A factor of the form \(e^{i\theta}\) is called a phase. Because \(|e^{i\theta}| = 1\), multiplying a single amplitude by a phase never changes that amplitude's magnitude - and so, if it were the only amplitude, it would never change any probability. This is why an overall global phase is physically invisible.

The subtlety - and the entire engine of quantum computing - is the relative phase between two amplitudes. Consider adding two amplitudes of equal size but different phase:

\[ \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(1\big) + \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(e^{i\theta}\big). \]

When \(\theta = 0\) the two terms reinforce; when \(\theta = \pi\) we have \(e^{i\pi} = -1\) and they cancel exactly, giving zero. The magnitudes never changed, yet the outcome swung from full to nothing. That switch - controlled purely by phase - is interference, and it is what a later lesson builds every quantum algorithm from.

Key idea

For an amplitude \(z\), the physically meaningful quantity is \(|z|^2 = a^2 + b^2\), a real non-negative number. A phase \(e^{i\theta}\) has magnitude 1 and so is invisible on its own, but a relative phase between amplitudes controls interference.

Key takeaways

  • The imaginary unit satisfies \(i^2 = -1\); a complex number is \(z = a + bi\).
  • The conjugate is \(z^* = a - bi\), and \(|z|^2 = z z^* = a^2 + b^2\) is real and non-negative.
  • Euler's formula: \(e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta\), with \(|e^{i\theta}| = 1\) for all real \(\theta\).
  • A phase is invisible alone but decisive as a relative phase, where it drives interference.
