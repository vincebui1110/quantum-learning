Every earlier module treated a qubit as a clean mathematical object: a vector \(\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\) that we rotate and measure at will. This module comes down to earth. A qubit has to be built out of real matter, and matter is stubborn. This lesson surveys the four leading ways people make physical qubits today, how each one encodes \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\), and the honest trade-offs between them. There is no winner yet - that is the whole point.

What every physical qubit needs

Whatever the technology, a usable qubit must offer the same four things: two clean, addressable quantum states to serve as \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\); a way to prepare, rotate, and entangle those states (gates); a way to read the state out; and enough isolation from the environment that the delicate superposition survives long enough to compute. Hold those four requirements in mind - each platform below satisfies them differently, and each pays a different price.

Superconducting transmons

The most industrially mature approach - used by Google and IBM - cools a small superconducting circuit to near absolute zero (around 15 millikelvin). At that temperature the circuit behaves like an artificial atom: an anharmonic oscillator whose energy levels are unevenly spaced. The lowest two levels become \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\); the uneven spacing lets microwave pulses address that bottom transition without accidentally climbing higher.

Why "anharmonic" matters

A perfect harmonic oscillator has equally spaced levels, so any pulse that drives \(|0\rangle\to|1\rangle\) would equally drive \(|1\rangle\to|2\rangle\) - you could never keep the qubit inside two levels. The transmon's slight anharmonicity is what makes it a usable qubit.

Pros: extremely fast gates (tens of nanoseconds), built with familiar chip-fabrication techniques, and the most qubits demonstrated on a single processor so far. Cons: short coherence (roughly a millisecond at best today), a demanding dilution-refrigerator, and limited connectivity - a qubit typically talks only to its near neighbours on the chip.

Trapped ions

Here the qubit is a single charged atom (such as ytterbium or calcium) suspended in a vacuum by electromagnetic fields. \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) are two internal electronic energy levels of the ion, controlled with laser or microwave pulses.

Pros: every ion is identical (nature manufactures them), coherence times are very long (seconds, sometimes far more), and any ion in a trap can interact with any other - all-to-all connectivity, which many algorithms love. Cons: gates are much slower than superconducting ones, and scaling to very large numbers of ions in one trap is hard, pushing designs toward networks of smaller traps.

Neutral atoms

A cousin of ion traps: neutral atoms are held in a grid of optical tweezers (tightly focused laser beams). \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) are again atomic states, and two-qubit gates are switched on by exciting atoms to large, strongly-interacting Rydberg states. Pros: hundreds of atoms arranged in flexible 2D and 3D patterns, long coherence, and reconfigurable connectivity. Cons: a younger technology, with gate fidelities and readout still maturing.

Photonics

Photonic qubits encode information in single particles of light - for example in a photon's polarization (horizontal = \(|0\rangle\), vertical = \(|1\rangle\)) or which of two paths it travels. Pros: photons barely interact with the environment (so little decoherence), fly at light speed, and are the natural carrier for quantum communication. Cons: that same weak interaction makes two-photon gates hard; many schemes are probabilistic and lean heavily on producing and detecting single photons reliably.

Read numbers with care

The figures above are order-of-magnitude and move every year as records are broken. Treat them as the shape of the trade-offs, not fixed constants. The one durable lesson is that no platform dominates on all axes at once.

Key takeaways

  • A physical qubit needs two clean states, controllable gates, readout, and isolation from noise.
  • Superconducting transmons use the lowest two levels of an anharmonic circuit: fast gates, short coherence, limited connectivity.
  • Trapped ions use internal atomic levels: long coherence and all-to-all connectivity, but slower gates.
  • Neutral atoms (Rydberg gates) and photonics (polarization/path) each bring their own strengths and open problems.
  • There is no universally best modality - each trades speed, coherence, connectivity, and scalability differently.
