A single gate rarely does anything interesting on its own. Real quantum circuits apply a sequence of gates to a qubit, one after another. This lesson explains how to compute the result of a sequence - and why the order in which you apply the gates genuinely changes the answer.

Sequencing = matrix multiplication

Suppose you apply gate \(A\) to \(|\psi\rangle\), then apply gate \(B\) to the result. Step by step:

\[ |\psi\rangle \;\xrightarrow{A}\; A|\psi\rangle \;\xrightarrow{B}\; B\big(A|\psi\rangle\big) = (BA)\,|\psi\rangle. \]

So the combined effect of "\(A\) then \(B\)" is the single matrix \(BA\) - the product of the gate matrices. Composing gates is nothing more than multiplying their matrices.

Watch the order: right to left

The gate applied first sits on the right of the product, closest to the state \(|\psi\rangle\). You read a circuit left to right (in time order), but you write the matrix product right to left. "\(A\) then \(B\)" is \(BA\), not \(AB\). This reversal trips up nearly everyone at first.

Reading a one-qubit circuit

Here is a two-gate circuit. The qubit enters on the left as \(|0\rangle\), passes through \(X\), then through \(H\):

|0⟩ ──[ X ]──[ H ]──  final state

Time flows left to right, so \(X\) acts first and \(H\) second. The final state is therefore \(H\,(X|0\rangle) = (HX)|0\rangle\) - note \(H\) on the left because it acts last.

Worked example: order matters

Circuit 1 - \(X\) then \(H\), applied to \(|0\rangle\). Work inside out:

\[ X|0\rangle = |1\rangle, \qquad H|1\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle) = |-\rangle. \]

So \((HX)|0\rangle = |-\rangle\).

Circuit 2 - \(H\) then \(X\) (the same two gates, reversed):

\[ H|0\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) = |+\rangle, \qquad X|+\rangle = |+\rangle. \]

So \((XH)|0\rangle = |+\rangle\). The two circuits use the identical gates yet reach different states, \(|-\rangle\) versus \(|+\rangle\). Order is not a bookkeeping detail; it is physics.

Why order matters: matrices don't commute

The reason is that matrix multiplication is generally non-commutative: \(BA \neq AB\) in general. We can see it directly for \(H\) and \(X\):

\[ HX = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \] \[ XH = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \]

These matrices are different, so \(HX \neq XH\), matching the two different physical outcomes above. Some pairs of gates do commute (for example \(Z\) and \(S\), both diagonal), but you can never assume it - always respect the given order.

Key idea

To find the final state of a circuit, multiply the gate matrices in reverse time order and apply the product to the input: for "\(A\), then \(B\), then \(C\)," compute \(CBA|\psi\rangle\). Because matrices generally do not commute, the sequence matters.

Building up longer circuits

The same rule extends to any length. For gates \(G_1, G_2, \ldots, G_k\) applied in that time order, the whole circuit is one unitary

\[ U = G_k \cdots G_2\, G_1, \]

and the output is \(U|\psi\rangle\). You can either multiply all the matrices first to get \(U\), then apply it once, or apply the gates one at a time to the evolving state - both give the same answer. This is the entire computational engine of a single-qubit circuit: a product of unitaries acting on a state vector.

Key takeaways

  • Applying gates in sequence multiplies their matrices: "\(A\) then \(B\)" is the matrix \(BA\).
  • Read circuits left to right in time, but write the product right to left - the first gate is rightmost.
  • Order matters because matrices generally don't commute: \(HX \neq XH\), giving \((HX)|0\rangle=|-\rangle\) but \((XH)|0\rangle=|+\rangle\).
  • A whole one-qubit circuit is a single unitary \(U = G_k\cdots G_1\), and its output is \(U|\psi\rangle\).
