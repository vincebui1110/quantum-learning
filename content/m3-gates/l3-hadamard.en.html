If you had to name a single gate as the emblem of quantum computing, it would be the Hadamard gate, written \(H\). It is the gate that turns definiteness into superposition and back again. Almost every quantum algorithm begins with a layer of Hadamards, because they are what put a qubit into the "both at once" state that quantum parallelism exploits.

The matrix

\[ H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}. \]

The factor \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\) is there to keep it unitary (and states normalized). Notice \(H\) is real and symmetric, and equal to its own conjugate transpose, so \(H^{\dagger} = H\).

What it does to the basis states

Applying \(H\) to the computational basis produces the two equal superpositions you met in the last module:

\[ H|0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) = |+\rangle, \qquad H|1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle) = |-\rangle. \]

This is the crucial move: a qubit sitting definitely at \(|0\rangle\) is transformed into a balanced blend of \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\). The only difference between \(H|0\rangle\) and \(H|1\rangle\) is the sign in front of \(|1\rangle\) - the relative phase - which is exactly the information the earlier lessons said measurement in the standard basis cannot see.

Worked example

Compute \(H|1\rangle\) explicitly, with \(|1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}\):

\[ H|1\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle) = |-\rangle. \]

The top entry is \(1\cdot 0 + 1\cdot 1 = 1\); the bottom is \(1\cdot 0 + (-1)\cdot 1 = -1\). The result is \(|-\rangle\), normalized and carrying the tell-tale minus sign.

Hadamard is its own inverse

A remarkable and useful fact: applying \(H\) twice does nothing. Because \(H^{\dagger} = H\) and \(H\) is unitary,

\[ H^2 = H^{\dagger}H = I. \]

Concretely, \(H\) undoes itself. Send \(|0\rangle\) to \(|+\rangle\), then apply \(H\) again:

\[ H|+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(H|0\rangle + H|1\rangle\big) = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|+\rangle + |-\rangle\big) = |0\rangle. \]

The \(|1\rangle\) parts of \(|+\rangle\) and \(|-\rangle\) cancel while the \(|0\rangle\) parts reinforce. This is interference in action - and it is the sharpest evidence that a superposition is not just "we don't know which." A classical coin flipped twice stays random; a qubit sent through two Hadamards returns to certainty.

Key idea

\(H\) is the gate that creates superposition (\(|0\rangle \to |+\rangle\)) and, applied again, removes it (\(|+\rangle \to |0\rangle\)). It moves between the standard basis \(\{|0\rangle,|1\rangle\}\) and the "diagonal" basis \(\{|+\rangle,|-\rangle\}\).

Why it matters

Because \(H\) maps a single definite state to an equal superposition, one Hadamard on each of \(n\) qubits creates a superposition over all \(2^n\) possible bit-strings at once. That is the launching pad for algorithms like Deutsch-Jozsa, Grover, and Shor: put everything into superposition with Hadamards, let interference do the computation, then measure. On the Bloch sphere, \(H\) is a 180° rotation about the diagonal axis halfway between x and z, which is why it swaps the roles of the \(x\)- and \(z\)-directions - turning phase information into measurable bit information and vice versa.

Key takeaways

  • \(H = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\) is the Hadamard gate.
  • \(H|0\rangle = |+\rangle\) and \(H|1\rangle = |-\rangle\): it creates equal superpositions.
  • \(H\) is self-inverse, \(H^2 = I\), so \(H|+\rangle = |0\rangle\) - a second \(H\) removes the superposition by interference.
  • Hadamards are the standard way to launch quantum algorithms into superposition over all inputs.
