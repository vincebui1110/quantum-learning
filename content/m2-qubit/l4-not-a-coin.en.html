It is tempting to shrink all of this into a comfortable classical picture: "a qubit in superposition is really already 0 or 1, we just don't know which until we look - like a coin under a cup." This lesson dismantles that picture, because seeing exactly why it fails is what makes the rest of the course meaningful.

The coin-under-a-cup model

The classical story is one of ignorance: the coin has a definite value; measurement merely reveals a fact that was already settled. If that were all a qubit is, quantum computing would offer nothing new - a probabilistic classical computer already models "unknown but definite" bits perfectly well.

The experiment that breaks it: interference

Here is a two-step process you can run (and try yourself in the Practice Lab). Start with a definite \(|0\rangle\). Apply a Hadamard gate \(H\), producing the equal superposition:

\[ H\,|0\rangle = |+\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|0\rangle + |1\rangle\big). \]

If you measured now, you would get 0 or 1 with equal probability - so far, consistent with the coin story. But don't measure. Apply a second Hadamard. Because \(H\) is its own inverse (\(H^2 = I\)):

\[ H\,|+\rangle = H H\,|0\rangle = |0\rangle. \]

Now measure: you get \(0\) with certainty. The randomness vanished.

Why this is fatal to the coin model

If the qubit after the first \(H\) were secretly a definite 0 or 1 (a coin already landed), the second \(H\) could not un-randomize it - a fair coin flipped, hidden, then "flipped" again is still 50/50. Certainty re-emerging proves the qubit was not secretly one value. Both amplitudes were genuinely present, and the second \(H\) let them interfere: the paths leading to \(|1\rangle\) cancelled, the paths to \(|0\rangle\) reinforced.

What made cancellation possible

Interference works only because amplitudes carry signs (and, in general, complex phases). In the second Hadamard, the \(|1\rangle\) outcome receives contributions of \(+\tfrac{1}{2}\) and \(-\tfrac{1}{2}\), which sum to zero. Probabilities - being non-negative - can never cancel like this. This is the entire reason a qubit is more than a random bit: it is described by amplitudes, and amplitudes can destructively interfere.

Key idea

A qubit in superposition is not "secretly 0 or 1." Its two amplitudes are simultaneously real and can cancel or reinforce. Every quantum algorithm - Deutsch-Jozsa, Grover, Shor - is an orchestration of exactly this interference to make wrong answers cancel and right answers reinforce.

Key takeaways

  • The "secretly already 0 or 1" model is testable - and interference refutes it.
  • Applying \(H\) twice to \(|0\rangle\) returns \(|0\rangle\) with certainty; a hidden classical bit could never do this.
  • Interference is possible because amplitudes have signs/phases and can cancel; probabilities cannot.
  • Harnessed interference is the engine of every quantum algorithm you will meet later.
