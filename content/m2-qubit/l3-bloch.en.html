We said amplitudes are complex numbers, and promised that would matter. Here is where it pays off. A complex amplitude carries not just a magnitude but a phase, and phase is what separates a qubit's geometry - the famous Bloch sphere - from a boring line segment.

Global phase: the part that does not matter

Consider multiplying an entire state by a complex number of magnitude one, \(e^{i\gamma}\):

\[ |\psi\rangle \;\longrightarrow\; e^{i\gamma}|\psi\rangle. \]

Does any experiment notice? Measure in any basis: every outcome probability is a squared magnitude, and \(|e^{i\gamma}\alpha|^2 = |e^{i\gamma}|^2|\alpha|^2 = |\alpha|^2\), because \(|e^{i\gamma}|=1\). The phase cancels everywhere. A global phase is therefore physically meaningless - \(|\psi\rangle\) and \(e^{i\gamma}|\psi\rangle\) are the same state.

Relative phase: the part that does matter

Now compare \(|+\rangle\) and \(|-\rangle\):

\[ |+\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|0\rangle + |1\rangle\big), \qquad |-\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|0\rangle - |1\rangle\big). \]

The only difference is a minus sign on the \(|1\rangle\) term - a relative phase between the two amplitudes. In the computational basis they are indistinguishable: both give 50/50. But this phase is not a global phase you can factor out, and it is absolutely real. Apply a Hadamard gate (module 3) and \(|+\rangle \to |0\rangle\) while \(|-\rangle \to |1\rangle\) - opposite, certain outcomes. Relative phase is observable; global phase is not.

The distinction in one line

Multiply the whole state by \(e^{i\gamma}\): invisible. Change the phase of one amplitude relative to the other: physical, and detectable in the right basis.

Packaging it all: the Bloch sphere

A qubit has two complex amplitudes (four real numbers). Normalization removes one, and discarding the meaningless global phase removes another. That leaves exactly two real parameters - which is why every pure qubit state can be written as a point on the surface of a sphere:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \cos\!\tfrac{\theta}{2}\,|0\rangle + e^{i\varphi}\sin\!\tfrac{\theta}{2}\,|1\rangle, \qquad \theta \in [0,\pi],\ \varphi \in [0,2\pi). \]

Here \(\theta\) is the polar angle (how much \(|0\rangle\) vs \(|1\rangle\)) and \(\varphi\) is the azimuthal angle (the relative phase). The landmarks:

Point on sphereStateMeaning
North pole (\(\theta=0\))\(|0\rangle\)definite 0
South pole (\(\theta=\pi\))\(|1\rangle\)definite 1
Equator, \(\varphi=0\)\(|+\rangle\)equal, phase 0
Equator, \(\varphi=\pi\)\(|-\rangle\)equal, phase \(\pi\)
Equator, \(\varphi=\tfrac{\pi}{2}\)\(|{+}i\rangle\)equal, phase \(\tfrac{\pi}{2}\)

The Bloch sphere is not decoration: in module 3 you will see that every single-qubit gate is literally a rotation of this sphere. It turns abstract algebra into geometry you can picture - and you can rotate it yourself in the Practice Lab.

Key idea

A pure qubit state is two real parameters, \((\theta,\varphi)\), and lives on the surface of the Bloch sphere. Latitude \(\theta\) sets the measurement probabilities; longitude \(\varphi\) is the relative phase. Global phase is exactly the coordinate the sphere throws away.

Key takeaways

  • Global phase \(e^{i\gamma}\) multiplies the whole state and is physically undetectable.
  • Relative phase between amplitudes is real and observable in the right basis (e.g. after a Hadamard).
  • Every pure qubit state is a point on the Bloch sphere, \(\cos\tfrac{\theta}{2}|0\rangle + e^{i\varphi}\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}|1\rangle\).
  • \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) are the poles; equal superpositions live on the equator, separated by their phase \(\varphi\).
