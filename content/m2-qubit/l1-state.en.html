A classical bit is the simplest object in computing: it is either 0 or 1. A qubit - a quantum bit - is the simplest object in quantum computing, and it is genuinely richer. This lesson defines exactly what a qubit's state is, using the notation you will read for the rest of the course.

States as vectors: the ket

We write the two definite states of a qubit as \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\). The angled bracket notation \(|\;\rangle\) is called a ket (introduced by Paul Dirac); for now, just read \(|0\rangle\) as "the state zero." Formally these are two-dimensional column vectors:

\[ |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}. \]

The power of a qubit is that it is not limited to these two. A general qubit state is a superposition - a weighted combination:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha\,|0\rangle + \beta\,|1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix}. \]

The two numbers \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are called amplitudes. They are complex numbers, not merely fractions - a fact that becomes essential two lessons from now when we discuss phase. If complex numbers are rusty, module 1 has a short refresher; for this lesson it is enough to know that every complex number \(z\) has a magnitude \(|z|\), and \(|z|^2\) is a non-negative real number.

Notation

\(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) are called the computational basis (or standard basis). "Basis" means every qubit state can be written as a combination of these two, just as every point in a plane is some combination of two axes.

The one rule amplitudes must obey: normalization

Amplitudes are not free to be anything. They must satisfy the normalization condition:

\[ |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1. \]

The reason will be precise in the next lesson: \(|\alpha|^2\) is the probability of measuring the qubit and finding 0, and \(|\beta|^2\) the probability of finding 1. Probabilities of the only two possible outcomes must sum to \(1\). Notice this is a condition on the squares of the magnitudes, not on \(\alpha+\beta\) - a distinction people trip over constantly.

Worked example

Is \(|\psi\rangle = \tfrac{3}{5}|0\rangle + \tfrac{4}{5}|1\rangle\) a valid state? Check normalization:

\[ \left|\tfrac{3}{5}\right|^2 + \left|\tfrac{4}{5}\right|^2 = \tfrac{9}{25} + \tfrac{16}{25} = \tfrac{25}{25} = 1. \ \checkmark \]

Yes. Even though \(\tfrac{3}{5} + \tfrac{4}{5} = \tfrac{7}{5} \neq 1\), the state is valid, because it is the squared magnitudes that must sum to one.

The most important superposition: equal weight

Set \(\alpha = \beta = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\). This gives a state so common it has its own name:

\[ |+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\big(|0\rangle + |1\rangle\big). \]

Check it is normalized: \(\left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\right)^2 + \left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\right)^2 = \tfrac12 + \tfrac12 = 1.\) The factor \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\) is exactly what is needed to keep the total probability at one. When people say a qubit is "both 0 and 1 at once," this balanced state is what they picture - but as the next two lessons show, that phrase is a loose summary of something more precise.

Key idea

A qubit's state is a two-dimensional complex vector \(\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\) with \(|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1\). The amplitudes \(\alpha,\beta\) are the complete description of the qubit - everything you can ever ask about it is computed from them.

Key takeaways

  • \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) are the two computational-basis states, written as column vectors.
  • A general state is a superposition \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\) with complex amplitudes.
  • Normalization requires \(|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1\) - a condition on squared magnitudes, not on the raw amplitudes.
  • \(|+\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)\) is the balanced, equal-weight superposition.
