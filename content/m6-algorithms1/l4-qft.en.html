The oracle algorithms so far used one round of Hadamards to interfere amplitudes. The quantum Fourier transform (QFT) is the far more powerful generalization of that step - a change of basis that reshuffles amplitudes according to their frequencies. It is the engine inside phase estimation and Shor's algorithm, so it earns its own lesson.

The classical Fourier transform, in one line

The discrete Fourier transform (DFT) takes a list of \(N\) numbers \(a_0,\dots,a_{N-1}\) and produces a new list of the same length,

\[ b_k = \frac{1}{\sqrt N}\sum_{x=0}^{N-1} a_x\, e^{2\pi i\, xk/N}, \]

revealing which frequencies are present in the original data. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) computes all \(N\) outputs in \(O(N\log N)\) arithmetic operations - already excellent, and the backbone of modern signal processing.

The quantum version: transform the amplitudes

The QFT does the very same arithmetic, but on the \(2^n = N\) amplitudes of an \(n\)-qubit state. Its action on a computational basis state \(|x\rangle\) is defined by

\[ \text{QFT}\,|x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} e^{2\pi i\, xk/N}\,|k\rangle, \qquad N = 2^n. \]

The phases \(e^{2\pi i\, xk/N}\) are precisely the DFT kernel above. By linearity, applied to a general state \(\sum_x a_x|x\rangle\), the QFT sends the amplitude list \((a_x)\) to its discrete Fourier transform \((b_k)\) - it Fourier-transforms the amplitudes themselves.

Worked example: the single-qubit QFT is just Hadamard

Take \(n=1\), so \(N=2\). The formula gives \(\text{QFT}|x\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\sum_{k=0}^{1} e^{2\pi i\, xk/2}|k\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\sum_k (-1)^{xk}|k\rangle\). For \(x=0\): \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)=|+\rangle\). For \(x=1\): \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle-|1\rangle)=|-\rangle\). That is exactly the Hadamard gate. The QFT on one qubit is \(H\) - so every Hadamard layer you have already met was a baby Fourier transform.

The product form and the gate count

A remarkable fact makes the QFT efficient: the big entangled-looking sum factors into a product of single-qubit states, each holding one binary digit of \(x\) in its phase:

\[ \text{QFT}\,|x_1\dots x_n\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt N}\bigotimes_{j=1}^{n}\Big(|0\rangle + e^{2\pi i\, 0.x_j x_{j+1}\dots x_n}|1\rangle\Big), \]

where \(0.x_j x_{j+1}\dots\) is a binary fraction. This product structure is what lets a circuit of Hadamards and controlled-phase rotations build the QFT with only

\[ O(n^2)\ \text{gates}. \]

Contrast the counts. The classical FFT needs \(O(N\log N) = O(n\,2^n)\) operations to transform \(N=2^n\) numbers; the QFT needs only \(O(n^2)\) gates for the same-sized transform - an exponential edge in gate count.

The catch: you cannot read the spectrum

That exponential gate advantage does not mean you get the Fourier coefficients for free. After the QFT the amplitudes \(b_k\) live inside the quantum state, but measurement only samples one outcome \(k\) with probability \(|b_k|^2\) - it never hands you the numbers \(b_k\) themselves. So the QFT is not a drop-in replacement for the FFT: it is a subroutine that must be embedded in a larger algorithm designed so that the measurement reveals something useful (a period, a phase). Phase estimation, next lesson, is the classic example.

Key idea

The QFT is the DFT acting on quantum amplitudes: \(|x\rangle \to \tfrac{1}{\sqrt N}\sum_k e^{2\pi i xk/N}|k\rangle\). It runs in \(O(n^2)\) gates - exponentially fewer than the FFT - but you still have to measure, so it is a building block, not a spectrum reader.

Key takeaways

  • The QFT Fourier-transforms the amplitudes of a state: \(|x\rangle \to \tfrac{1}{\sqrt N}\sum_k e^{2\pi i xk/N}|k\rangle\), \(N=2^n\).
  • On one qubit the QFT is exactly the Hadamard gate.
  • A product form lets a circuit implement it in \(O(n^2)\) gates, vs \(O(N\log N)\) for the classical FFT.
  • Measurement only samples the transformed state - the QFT does not by itself give a readable Fourier spectrum.
