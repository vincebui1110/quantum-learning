We now assemble the pieces - phase kickback and the inverse QFT - into the single most important subroutine in quantum computing: quantum phase estimation (QPE). It is the beating heart of Shor's factoring algorithm and of quantum chemistry energy calculations. This lesson is conceptual, but the key equations tell the whole story.

The problem: read an eigenvalue's phase

Suppose you have a unitary \(U\) and one of its eigenstates \(|u\rangle\). Because \(U\) is unitary, its eigenvalues are pure phases, so we can write

\[ U\,|u\rangle = e^{2\pi i\varphi}\,|u\rangle, \qquad 0 \le \varphi < 1. \]

The number \(\varphi\) is the eigenphase. Applying \(U\) does not change the state \(|u\rangle\) in any observable way on its own - the phase \(e^{2\pi i\varphi}\) is global and invisible to measurement. Phase estimation's job is to drag that hidden \(\varphi\) out into a register you can read, to \(n\) bits of precision.

The engine: kickback with controlled powers of U

Take a counting register of \(n\) qubits (which will hold the answer) plus a register holding \(|u\rangle\). Put every counting qubit into \(|+\rangle\) with Hadamards, then apply a controlled-\(U\) from each counting qubit, but with the powers doubling: qubit \(j\) controls \(U^{2^{j}}\).

Here kickback strikes again. Since \(|u\rangle\) is an eigenstate, applying \(U^{2^j}\) multiplies it by \(e^{2\pi i\,2^{j}\varphi}\); with the control in superposition, that phase kicks back onto the control qubit:

\[ \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|0\rangle + |1\rangle\big) \;\longrightarrow\; \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|0\rangle + e^{2\pi i\, 2^{j}\varphi}|1\rangle\big). \]

The state \(|u\rangle\) itself is never disturbed - it just supplies the eigenphase over and over. After all the controlled powers, the counting register holds

\[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi i\,\varphi k}\,|k\rangle. \]
Look familiar?

Compare this to the QFT formula \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt N}\sum_k e^{2\pi i\, xk/N}|k\rangle\). It is exactly the Fourier transform of the basis state \(|x\rangle\) where \(x/N = \varphi\), i.e. \(x = 2^n\varphi\). The controlled powers have prepared the Fourier transform of the answer.

The decoder: inverse QFT

If the phases encode the QFT of the number \(x = 2^n\varphi\), then running the QFT backwards must recover \(|x\rangle\). That is the final step: apply the inverse QFT to the counting register, then measure. When \(\varphi\) is exactly an \(n\)-bit fraction, the outcome is the integer \(x = 2^n\varphi\), and dividing by \(2^n\) gives \(\varphi\) exactly:

\[ \text{measure} \;\Rightarrow\; x, \qquad \varphi \approx \frac{x}{2^n}. \]

When \(\varphi\) is not an exact \(n\)-bit fraction, the inverse QFT peaks the probability sharply on the nearest \(n\)-bit approximation, so measurement returns the best \(n\)-bit estimate with high probability. More counting qubits buy more bits of precision.

Sanity check: a phase that is exactly representable

Let \(n=3\) and \(\varphi = 0.101_2 = 5/8\). The controlled powers write \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt8}\sum_k e^{2\pi i (5/8) k}|k\rangle\), which is precisely \(\text{QFT}|5\rangle\). The inverse QFT maps it back to \(|5\rangle = |101\rangle\), measured with certainty. Read off \(\varphi = 5/8\). No sampling noise, because the answer fit exactly in 3 bits.

Why it matters: Shor and chemistry

Phase estimation is the reason quantum computers threaten RSA. Shor's factoring algorithm reduces factoring to finding the period of the modular-exponentiation map, and that period is exactly an eigenphase of a unitary you can build - QPE extracts it, and the QFT inside makes it efficient. The same subroutine estimates the ground-state energy of a molecule: encode the Hamiltonian's time-evolution as \(U = e^{-iHt}\), prepare an approximate eigenstate, and QPE reads off the energy eigenvalue. Two of the field's flagship applications are, at their core, this one circuit.

Key idea

Phase estimation = (Hadamards) + (controlled-\(U^{2^j}\) that kick the eigenphase into the counting register as a QFT) + (inverse QFT to decode) + (measure). It converts an invisible eigenphase \(\varphi\) into a readable \(n\)-bit number, and it is the core of Shor's algorithm and quantum chemistry.

Key takeaways

  • QPE estimates the phase \(\varphi\) in \(U|u\rangle = e^{2\pi i\varphi}|u\rangle\) to \(n\) bits of precision.
  • Controlled-\(U^{2^j}\) powers use phase kickback to write \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_k e^{2\pi i\varphi k}|k\rangle\) into the counting register - the QFT of the answer.
  • The inverse QFT decodes that into a measurable integer \(x \approx 2^n\varphi\); more qubits mean more precision.
  • QPE is the core subroutine behind Shor's factoring and quantum-chemistry energy estimation.
