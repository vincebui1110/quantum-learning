Entanglement is the feature that most sharply separates quantum from classical computing. It is often described in mystical terms, but the definition is precise and almost anticlimactic: an entangled state is simply one that cannot be factored into independent parts. This lesson makes that exact, then builds the most famous entangled states - the Bell states - from gates you already know.

Product states versus entangled states

A two-qubit state is a product state (or separable) if it can be written as a tensor product of two single-qubit states:

\[ |\psi\rangle = |a\rangle \otimes |b\rangle. \]

In a product state each qubit has its own definite state; describing them separately loses nothing. A state that is not separable - one for which no choice of \(|a\rangle,|b\rangle\) works - is called entangled. For an entangled pair, neither qubit has a state of its own; only the pair, together, is fully described.

A quick separability test

Expand \(|a\rangle\otimes|b\rangle = (a_0|0\rangle+a_1|1\rangle)(b_0|0\rangle+b_1|1\rangle)\). The amplitudes of \(|00\rangle,|01\rangle,|10\rangle,|11\rangle\) are \(a_0b_0,\ a_0b_1,\ a_1b_0,\ a_1b_1\). A product state must therefore satisfy \(\alpha\delta = \beta\gamma\) (both equal \(a_0b_0a_1b_1\)). If \(\alpha\delta \neq \beta\gamma\), the state is entangled.

Deriving the Bell state, step by step

Take the standard Bell circuit: start in \(|00\rangle\), apply a Hadamard to qubit 0, then a CNOT with qubit 0 as control and qubit 1 as target. Recall \(H|0\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)\).

Step 1 - Hadamard on qubit 0. The second qubit is untouched:

\[ |00\rangle \ \xrightarrow{\ H\otimes I\ }\ \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|0\rangle+|1\rangle\big)\otimes|0\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|00\rangle + |10\rangle\big). \]

Step 2 - CNOT (control 0, target 1). Apply the rule "flip target iff control = 1" to each term. The \(|00\rangle\) term has control 0, so it is unchanged; the \(|10\rangle\) term has control 1, so its target flips to give \(|11\rangle\):

\[ \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|00\rangle + |10\rangle\big) \ \xrightarrow{\ \mathrm{CNOT}\ }\ \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|00\rangle + |11\rangle\big) \equiv |\Phi^+\rangle. \]

Is \(|\Phi^+\rangle\) entangled? Its amplitudes are \(\alpha=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2},\ \beta=0,\ \gamma=0,\ \delta=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\). The separability test needs \(\alpha\delta=\beta\gamma\), but here \(\alpha\delta = \tfrac12 \neq 0 = \beta\gamma\). No factorization exists: \(|\Phi^+\rangle\) is entangled. Two ordinary gates, acting on an unentangled input, have produced genuine entanglement.

The four Bell states

Feeding the same H-then-CNOT circuit the four different inputs \(|00\rangle,|01\rangle,|10\rangle,|11\rangle\) produces four maximally entangled states, the Bell basis:

\[ |\Phi^{\pm}\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|00\rangle \pm |11\rangle\big), \qquad |\Psi^{\pm}\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|01\rangle \pm |10\rangle\big). \]

These four states are mutually orthogonal and form a complete basis for the two-qubit space - a basis made entirely of entangled states, in which no single qubit has a definite value.

Correlation without a hidden pre-set value

Measure the first qubit of \(|\Phi^+\rangle\) in the standard basis. Because only \(|00\rangle\) and \(|11\rangle\) appear, the outcome is 50/50, but whatever you get, the second qubit is instantly determined to match: 0 forces 0, 1 forces 1. The two results are perfectly correlated.

Why this is more than a classical correlation

Two gloves in two boxes are also perfectly correlated: open one, learn the other. But the gloves had fixed identities all along. The Bell pair does not - its correlations hold in every measurement basis at once, a stronger constraint no fixed hidden values can reproduce. Module 5 turns this into the Bell inequality, an experiment that rules out the "hidden pre-set value" picture entirely.

Common misconception

The instant determination of the far qubit does not transmit information. Your local outcome is random and uncontrollable, so you cannot use it to send a message. Entanglement gives correlation, not communication - the theme of the next lesson.

Key idea

A state is entangled when it cannot be written as \(|a\rangle\otimes|b\rangle\). Applying \(H\) then CNOT to \(|00\rangle\) yields the entangled Bell state \(|\Phi^+\rangle=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|00\rangle+|11\rangle)\), in which measuring one qubit instantly fixes the other.

Key takeaways

  • A product (separable) state factors as \(|a\rangle\otimes|b\rangle\); an entangled state does not.
  • Separability test: a product state needs \(\alpha\delta=\beta\gamma\); \(|\Phi^+\rangle\) fails it, so it is entangled.
  • \(H\) on qubit 0 then CNOT(0\(\to\)1) turns \(|00\rangle\) into \(|\Phi^+\rangle=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|00\rangle+|11\rangle)\).
  • The four Bell states \(|\Phi^\pm\rangle,|\Psi^\pm\rangle\) form an orthonormal basis of maximally entangled states.
  • Measuring one qubit determines the other, but this correlation cannot send a signal.
