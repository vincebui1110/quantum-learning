Grover's quadratic speed-up is nice; Shor's algorithm (Peter Shor, 1994) is the earthquake. It factors an \(n\)-bit integer in time polynomial in \(n\) - an exponential speed-up over the best known classical methods. Because the security of RSA and much of the internet rests on factoring being hard, Shor turned quantum computing from a curiosity into a subject that governments and standards bodies plan around. This lesson traces the reduction from factoring to period-finding and is honest about what today's hardware can and cannot do.

The key reduction: factoring becomes period-finding

We want to factor a composite \(N\). Pick a random integer \(a\) with \(1 < a < N\). First compute \(\gcd(a, N)\) classically (fast, via Euclid's algorithm). If it is not \(1\), we already found a factor by luck - done. Otherwise \(a\) is coprime to \(N\), and we study the function

\[ f(x) = a^x \bmod N. \]

This function is periodic: there is a smallest positive integer \(r\), the order of \(a\), with \(a^r \equiv 1 \pmod N\), so \(f(x+r) = f(x)\). Finding \(r\) is the whole ball game. Given an even \(r\) with \(a^{r/2} \not\equiv -1 \pmod N\), we have

\[ a^r - 1 \equiv 0 \;\Rightarrow\; (a^{r/2}-1)(a^{r/2}+1) \equiv 0 \pmod N, \]

so \(\gcd(a^{r/2}-1,\,N)\) and \(\gcd(a^{r/2}+1,\,N)\) are (with good probability) non-trivial factors of \(N\). Both gcds are classical. The only hard step is finding the period \(r\).

Key idea

Shor does not factor directly. It reduces factoring to finding the period of \(a^x \bmod N\), then solves that period-finding problem exponentially faster than any known classical method - using quantum phase estimation and the Quantum Fourier Transform.

The quantum part: period-finding via phase estimation and the QFT

Classically, finding \(r\) is as hard as factoring itself - you would essentially have to evaluate \(f\) until it repeats. Quantumly we exploit interference. Prepare a superposition over many inputs \(x\), compute \(a^x \bmod N\) into a second register, and the input register is left in a state whose amplitudes are periodic with period \(r\). Applying the Quantum Fourier Transform then concentrates amplitude on values related to \(1/r\):

\[ \text{QFT}:\quad |x\rangle \longmapsto \frac{1}{\sqrt{M}}\sum_{y=0}^{M-1} e^{2\pi i\,xy/M}\,|y\rangle. \]

The QFT is exactly the tool that turns a periodic pattern into sharp peaks - the quantum analogue of reading off a frequency. This is the same machinery as quantum phase estimation applied to the operator "multiply by \(a\) mod \(N\)": its eigenphases encode \(r\). Measuring the transformed register yields a value \(y\) close to a multiple of \(M/r\).

Back to classical: continued fractions

The measured \(y\) gives an approximation \(y/M \approx k/r\) for some unknown integer \(k\). To extract \(r\) from this ratio we run the continued fractions algorithm classically, which recovers the fraction \(k/r\) in lowest terms and hence a candidate \(r\). We check whether \(a^r \equiv 1\); if not (or if \(r\) is odd, or \(a^{r/2}\equiv -1\)), we retry with a new random \(a\). A few repetitions succeed with high probability.

Worked example (small)

Factor \(N = 15\) with \(a = 7\). Compute the powers mod 15: \(7^1=7,\ 7^2=49\equiv 4,\ 7^3\equiv 13,\ 7^4\equiv 1\). The period is \(r = 4\) (even). Then

\[ a^{r/2} = 7^2 = 49 \equiv 4 \pmod{15}, \] \[ \gcd(4-1,15)=\gcd(3,15)=3, \qquad \gcd(4+1,15)=\gcd(5,15)=5. \]

So \(15 = 3 \times 5\). Everything here except the period-finding is elementary classical arithmetic; the quantum computer's sole job was to hand us \(r = 4\).

Why this matters: RSA and post-quantum cryptography

RSA encryption is secure precisely because factoring a large \(N\) (a product of two big primes) is believed classically intractable - the best known classical algorithm, the general number field sieve, runs in sub-exponential but super-polynomial time. Shor's polynomial-time factoring would break RSA outright, and a close cousin breaks elliptic-curve cryptography (ECC) via the discrete-logarithm problem. This is why standards bodies (e.g. NIST) have been selecting post-quantum cryptography - classical algorithms based on problems (like lattices) with no known quantum speed-up.

Reality check

Shor's algorithm is proven correct, but running it on a cryptographically relevant key is far beyond today's hardware. Breaking 2048-bit RSA is estimated to need on the order of millions of physical qubits with full error correction; current devices have at most hundreds to a few thousand noisy qubits, and public demonstrations factor only tiny numbers like 15 or 21. The threat is why "harvest now, decrypt later" is taken seriously - but no real key has been broken.

History

Peter Shor announced the algorithm in 1994 at AT&T Bell Labs. It was the first convincing example of an exponential quantum advantage for a problem people deeply cared about, and it single-handedly turned quantum computing into a serious, well-funded field.

Key takeaways

  • Shor reduces factoring \(N\) to finding the period \(r\) of \(a^x \bmod N\); gcd and continued fractions are the classical glue.
  • The quantum core is period-finding via phase estimation and the Quantum Fourier Transform - an exponential speed-up over the best known classical factoring.
  • Given an even period with \(a^{r/2}\not\equiv -1\), the factors are \(\gcd(a^{r/2}\pm 1,\,N)\).
  • Shor threatens RSA and ECC, driving post-quantum cryptography - but current hardware cannot yet factor real keys.
