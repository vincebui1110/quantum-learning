What Is a Qubit?

The strange two-state object that replaces the classical bit - and why it can be more than one thing at once.

Read lesson

A classical bit is a switch: it is either 0 or 1, full stop. A qubit is a quantum system, built from things like a trapped ion, a superconducting loop, or a photon, that can be prepared, nudged with operations, and finally measured. Before that final measurement, its state is not simply "0" or "1" - it is described by a combination of both, with each one carrying a certain weight.

A useful (if imperfect) picture: a coin spinning in the air is not yet heads or tails - it only becomes one or the other the instant it lands and you look. A qubit works something like that spinning coin. The next few lessons build up exactly what "combination of both" means, and why it is not just a fancy way of saying "we don't know yet."

Superposition, Plainly

A working mental model for "both at once" that doesn't require a physics degree to hold onto.

Read lesson

Superposition is the precise term for that "combination of both" state. A qubit's state is described by two numbers, called amplitudes, one attached to the possibility of measuring 0 and one attached to measuring 1. Squaring the size of an amplitude gives the probability of seeing that outcome when you finally measure.

It's tempting to think superposition just means "the answer is secretly already decided, we just don't know which" - like a coin under a cup. It's genuinely stranger than that: those two amplitudes can reinforce or cancel each other out in ways a hidden, already-decided value never could. That extra behavior, called interference, is what later lessons in the Wave Lab build real algorithms out of.

Bits vs. Qubits

Where classical computing ends and quantum computing begins, laid out side by side.

Read lesson

A classical bit is deterministic and copyable: read it as many times as you like, it always says the same thing, and you can duplicate it freely. A qubit's measurement outcome is probabilistic, and a qubit generally cannot be copied exactly without disturbing it - a rule physicists call the no-cloning theorem.

The bigger difference shows up when you have many of them together. N classical bits are always in exactly one of 2 to the N possible patterns at a time. N qubits can be placed into a state that involves all 2 to the N patterns at once. That richer space is the raw resource quantum algorithms use - though the moment you measure, you still only get N ordinary classical bits back out, which is why designing a good quantum algorithm is really about arranging for the right answer to be the one you're likely to see.

The Double-Slit Experiment

The century-old experiment that first hinted the universe runs on probability, not certainty.

Read lesson

Fire particles, one at a time, at a wall with two open slits, with a screen behind it to record where each one lands. Common sense says each particle goes through one slit or the other, landing in one of two clumps directly behind the slits. That is not what happens: over many particles, a striped interference pattern builds up on the screen, the same kind of pattern you'd get from overlapping water waves.

Even stranger: if you add a detector that tells you which slit each particle actually went through, the interference pattern vanishes, and you get the two plain clumps common sense expected. Simply having "which path" information available, even without a human checking it, is enough to destroy the pattern. This 100-year-old result is the clearest hint that quantum objects carry wave-like probability - and that watching too closely changes the outcome, a theme picked up again in the lesson on measurement and collapse.

Reading the Bloch Sphere

A simple picture for where a qubit's state "points" - no trigonometry required yet.

Read lesson

The Bloch sphere is a globe used to draw a single qubit's state as a point on its surface. The north pole represents the definite state "0," the south pole represents the definite state "1," and every other point on the surface represents some superposition of the two. How close a point sits to a pole tells you the probability of measuring that outcome.

There's a second piece of information baked into the picture: how far around the sphere (its longitude, roughly) the point sits, called its phase. Phase doesn't change the measurement probabilities of that qubit on its own, but it is exactly what interference reaches for later - two states with the same probabilities but different phases behave very differently once you start combining qubits. On real hardware, applying a gate to a qubit means physically rotating this point to a new position on the sphere.

Why Quantum Computers Exist

The problems classical computers are structurally bad at - and why that gap matters.

Read lesson

Certain problems get dramatically harder as they grow: simulating how electrons behave in a new battery material, finding the true minimum of a complicated tangled optimization, or factoring a very large number. For problems like these, the resources a classical computer needs can grow so fast that even a modest increase in size makes them practically unsolvable, no matter how much classical hardware you throw at it.

Quantum computers exist because superposition, interference, and entanglement (introduced in the next deck) offer a fundamentally different way to represent and process information for exactly this narrow class of problems. It is worth being precise about what that means: a quantum computer is not a faster calculator for everything, including most of what a phone or laptop does every day - it is a specialized tool aimed at the handful of problem types where that different representation pays off, several of which are named and explained in the Frontier Chamber.

Entanglement and Correlation

Two qubits, one shared fate - and why Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance."

Read lesson

Entanglement is a shared state between two or more qubits where the outcome of measuring one is tied to the outcome of measuring the other, however far apart they are, in a way no classical shared setup can reproduce. A simple but limited analogy: if a pair of gloves is split into two boxes and mailed to opposite sides of the world, opening one box and finding a left glove instantly tells you the other box holds the right one. That much is just ordinary correlation, decided in advance.

What makes entanglement different is that the correlations show up even when you measure the qubits in different, freely-chosen ways - and the pattern of results is stronger than any "decided in advance" explanation, classical gloves included, could produce. This is precisely what physicists have tested directly (in what are called Bell tests), which is why Einstein's skeptical nickname for it, "spooky action at a distance," turned out to describe something real rather than a misunderstanding.

Quantum Gates: X, H, CNOT

The small toolkit of operations that every quantum circuit is built from.

Read lesson

A gate is a small, reversible operation applied to one or more qubits - the quantum equivalent of AND, OR, and NOT in classical circuits, except every quantum gate must be reversible. The X gate is the simplest: it flips a qubit, turning a definite 0 into a definite 1 and back, exactly like a classical NOT.

The H gate (Hadamard) is the one that actually makes superposition: fed a definite 0 or 1, it outputs an even, evenly-weighted combination of both, becoming the go-to "start a superposition" move in almost every quantum circuit. CNOT is a two-qubit gate: it flips a target qubit only if a separate control qubit is currently 1. Applying H to one qubit and then CNOT from that qubit to a second is the standard, minimal recipe for creating entanglement - which is exactly the circuit built in the next lesson.

Interference and Amplitude

How quantum algorithms cancel out wrong answers before you ever take a measurement.

Read lesson

Recall from the Foundations Deck that a qubit's amplitudes can be positive or negative (in general, they're complex numbers, but sign is enough to build the intuition). When several paths through a circuit lead to the same final outcome, their amplitudes add together before you square them for a probability - and amplitudes with opposite signs can cancel each other out almost entirely. This cancellation, and its opposite (amplitudes reinforcing each other), is called interference, and it's the same phenomenon behind the double-slit experiment's stripes.

This is not a side effect - it is the actual mechanism every quantum algorithm relies on. A well-designed circuit is a carefully choreographed sequence of gates arranged so that the amplitude for every wrong answer interferes destructively toward zero, while the amplitude for the right answer interferes constructively and grows. Measure at the end, and the odds are stacked heavily toward walking away with the right answer.

Measurement and Collapse

What actually happens in the instant you look at a qubit's state.

Read lesson

Measuring a qubit forces it to commit to one definite outcome, 0 or 1, with a probability set by the squared size of its amplitude for that outcome. The instant this happens, the superposition is gone - the qubit is now simply in whichever state it was found in, and all the other amplitude information it was carrying is lost for good. This is called collapse, and unlike almost every other quantum operation, it cannot be undone.

This is exactly why interference has to do its work before that one irreversible measurement: once you look, the rich superposition that made the calculation possible is over. It's also why quantum programs are typically run many times - each run gives one probabilistic sample, and you build up confidence in the answer from the statistics across many shots, not from a single measurement.

Building Your First Circuit

Wiring gates together into a small, real, runnable quantum program.

Read lesson

Put the last few lessons together into an actual, if tiny, circuit. Start two qubits at a definite 0. Apply an H gate to the first qubit, putting it into superposition. Then apply a CNOT gate using that first qubit as the control and the second as the target. That's the whole circuit - and it produces what's called a Bell pair, the simplest entangled state there is.

Measure both qubits many times and a clear pattern appears in the results: you get "00" about half the time and "11" about half the time - never "01" or "10." Neither qubit's outcome is decided until you measure, but whichever way the first one falls, the second always matches it. In practice, a circuit like this is drawn as a small diagram of gates in order, run on real hardware or a simulator, and the results come back as counts of each outcome across many repetitions - the raw material every quantum algorithm is judged by.

Quantum Algorithms, an Overview

Deutsch-Jozsa and its relatives: the first proofs that quantum could outpace classical.

Read lesson

The Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm, from 1992, is the historical first proof that a quantum computer could solve a problem with a single query where every classical approach needs many queries in the worst case. The problem it solves is somewhat artificial (deciding whether a hidden function always gives the same output, or gives each output equally often), but that's exactly what makes it valuable as a teaching example: the speed-up comes purely from the interference trick in the previous two lessons, with no extra machinery to distract from it.

Deutsch-Jozsa itself doesn't solve a problem anyone urgently needs solved. What it proved mattered: that the superposition-plus-interference toolkit could give a real, provable advantage over classical computing, not just a theoretical curiosity. The Frontier Chamber picks up from here with algorithms aimed at problems people actually care about - factoring numbers and searching data.

Shor's Algorithm

The algorithm that threatens modern encryption by factoring large numbers exponentially faster.

Read lesson

Shor's algorithm finds the prime factors of a large number exponentially faster than the best classical methods known today. It works by reframing factoring as a period-finding problem, then using a quantum Fourier transform (a larger-scale version of the interference trick from the Wave Lab) to find that hidden period efficiently, something classical computers are believed to be structurally bad at.

This matters well beyond mathematics: much of today's public-key encryption, including the RSA scheme protecting a large share of internet traffic, is secure specifically because factoring large numbers is assumed to be hard for classical computers. A sufficiently large, fault-tolerant quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could in principle break it, which is exactly why "post-quantum cryptography" is already being standardized. Today's real quantum hardware, worth saying plainly, is nowhere near large or reliable enough to threaten real-world encryption keys yet.

Grover's Search

A quadratic speed-up for searching unsorted data - and exactly where that speed-up matters.

Read lesson

Grover's algorithm searches an unsorted list of N items for one that satisfies some checkable condition in roughly the square root of N steps, compared to checking about half the list, N over 2, on average classically. That's called a quadratic speed-up - real and provable, but far more modest than Shor's exponential one.

The practical reach of Grover's algorithm follows directly from that gap: it helps most on any problem that can be reframed as "find the input that passes a test," including some cryptographic brute-force search and certain optimization subproblems. Because the speed-up is only quadratic, doubling your problem size only requires roughly 40% more steps rather than doubling them - useful, but nowhere near as disruptive as an exponential speed-up would be.

Quantum Error Correction

Why real qubits are noisy, and the codes that keep information alive anyway.

Read lesson

Real qubits are fragile. Stray heat, electromagnetic noise, and unwanted interactions with their surroundings constantly nudge them, causing errors and a gradual loss of the delicate phase information (from the Bloch sphere lesson) that interference depends on - a process called decoherence. Left unchecked, this noise destroys a computation long before it finishes.

Quantum error correction spreads the information of one "logical," protected qubit across many physical qubits, arranged so that errors can be detected and fixed without ever directly measuring, and thereby destroying, the fragile quantum information itself. That constraint, correct errors without looking directly at the data, makes this a fundamentally harder problem than classical error correction, and it's the single biggest reason building a large, reliable ("fault-tolerant") quantum computer remains such a serious engineering challenge.

Variational Quantum Eigensolvers

Hybrid classical-quantum algorithms designed for today's imperfect hardware.

Read lesson

A Variational Quantum Eigensolver, or VQE, is a hybrid algorithm split across two machines. A modest quantum circuit prepares a trial state, using a set of adjustable parameters, and measures a quantity of interest from it - commonly the energy of a molecule being simulated. A classical computer then reads that measurement, nudges the parameters to try to lower the value, and sends the new parameters back to the quantum circuit, repeating the loop until the value stops improving.

This back-and-forth design exists for a practical reason: it's built to squeeze useful results out of today's noisy, error-prone quantum hardware, often called NISQ (noisy intermediate-scale quantum), rather than requiring the large, fully fault-tolerant machine that Shor's algorithm would need and that doesn't exist yet. That's why VQE and algorithms like it, not factoring, are where most real quantum hardware is actually being put to work right now.

Quantum Supremacy Experiments

What Google and others actually claimed to prove in 2019 - and what they didn't.

Read lesson

In 2019, Google's Sycamore processor ran a specific, deliberately contrived sampling task in a few minutes that the team estimated would take the best classical supercomputer of the time far longer to reproduce - a claim they called quantum supremacy (others have since run related experiments too). As a proof that quantum hardware can, on some task, genuinely outrun classical hardware, it held up.

What it did not show is worth being just as clear about: the task itself had no known practical use, chosen specifically because it favored quantum hardware, and later classical algorithm and hardware improvements have narrowed that original gap considerably. Read it for what it was - a scientific milestone proving the underlying physics works at scale - not as a sign that everyday, useful quantum advantage had arrived.

The Road to Fault Tolerance

What has to be true before quantum computers become everyday, dependable tools.

Read lesson

Getting from today's noisy machines to dependable, everyday quantum computers means clearing several stacked hurdles at once: many more physical qubits for every protected logical qubit under current error-correction overhead, substantially lower error rates per operation, better connectivity between qubits, and an industry-wide bet on hardware that hasn't settled yet - superconducting circuits, trapped ions, photonics, and neutral atoms are all still genuinely in the running.

The honest, unglamorous summary: progress has been steady for two decades and shows no sign of stopping, but it is measured in years, not months. Today's practical value from quantum computing mostly comes from research and hybrid approaches like VQE, run on modest, imperfect hardware - not from fault-tolerant machines running Shor's or Grover's algorithms at meaningful scale. That machine is the actual frontier this deck is named for.
